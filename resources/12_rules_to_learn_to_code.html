01
Rule One
Trick Your Brain with the 20 Minute Rule
+

Learning to code is like going to the gym. Even spending a whole weekend coding won't create a visible difference — but coding regularly will build your "coding muscles" over time.

The trick: as soon as you get home, tell yourself you'll code for just 20 minutes. Your brain won't resist 20 minutes. Once you start, inertia kicks in and you'll likely code for an hour or more.

To build the habit, use a monthly calendar. Mark each day you code with a line. Don't break the streak. That single unbroken line is surprisingly motivating.

TL;DR — Your ActionEvery day, open your laptop and code for at least 20 minutes. Just getting started is the hardest part.
02
Rule Two
Code for a Purpose
+

Don't learn to code for the sake of learning to code. Skills requiring a lot of practice need a deep internal motivation — something from within that makes you forget to eat and sleep.

Instead, start by following tutorials that actually make something. It could be a dice game, a flashcard app, or a to-do list. As long as you build something you can use and play with, you'll stay motivated.

App Brewery students have built: an app that wakes them up 1 minute earlier every day, a custom mother's day slideshow, a steak timer. The possibilities are limited only by your imagination.

TL;DR — Your ActionThink of ONE simple app you want to build. Keep it in mind throughout this course as your goal.
"You don't hire for skills, you hire for attitude. You can always teach skills." — Simon Sinek
03
Rule Three
There is No "Perfect" Language to Learn
+

People always ask: "Which programming language should I learn first?" — Python? JavaScript? The truth is, a programming language is just a tool. You pick the right tool for the job.

Making websites → JavaScript. Making iOS apps → Swift. Data science → Python. The good news: the core concepts (loops, functions, conditionals) are the same in every language. Only the syntax differs.

Since you want to build websites, JavaScript is your tool. But you're not locked in forever — once you understand programming thinking, learning new languages becomes much easier.

TL;DR — Your ActionStop wondering "what should I learn?" — you're learning HTML, CSS, JavaScript. That's the right choice for web development. Focus.
04
Rule Four
Understand What You're Writing
+

The worst trap in programming tutorials is copying code you don't understand. You type hundreds of lines, something breaks, and you have no idea why — because you never understood what any of it does.

Good learning has a ramp: knowledge builds on previous knowledge. If the ramp is too steep, you get lost. Too shallow, you get bored. Find tutorials that explain every single step.

If a tutorial uses the word "simply" a lot, or jumps from beginner to advanced without explanation — leave it. There are better tutorials.

TL;DR — Your ActionNever copy code you don't understand. If you paste something, read every line and ask: "Why is this here? What does this do?"
"You don't need more recipes. You need to learn to cook without them." — Tim Ferriss
05
Rule Five
It's OK to Not Know
+

Software engineers are the profession with the largest population of Imposter Syndrome sufferers — feeling like a fraud who doesn't really belong. Studies show 70% of people have felt this.

The truth? Even senior engineers at Google use Stack Overflow every single day. There are over 9,000 methods in iOS and a new web framework every week — nobody memorises all that.

What employers value is your ability to think and find answers — not your ability to memorise. Always try to figure it out yourself first, then Google the error message, then ask on Stack Overflow.

TL;DR — Your ActionWhen stuck: (1) Think yourself → (2) Google the error → (3) Stack Overflow. Never be ashamed to look things up.
"Don't be afraid to ask for help. Asking isn't a sign of weakness, it's a sign of strength." — Barack Obama
06
Rule Six
Be a Copycat
+

Lacking project ideas? Don't start from scratch — clone something that already exists. Make your own Notepad. Make your own calculator. Build Tetris. Build Minesweeper. Build a Flappy Bird clone.

By building copies of existing apps, you're walking a path that millions have walked before. This means when you get stuck, people online will be able to help you — because they've hit the same walls.

Books are good references, but building is how you actually learn.

TL;DR — Your ActionAfter each section of the course, pick one real-world app and try to clone a tiny piece of it using what you learned.
"Good artists borrow, great artists steal." — Pablo Picasso
07
Rule Seven
Be Accountable
+

The biggest problem with online courses? Zero accountability. Nobody cares if you don't finish. And we can always find a reason to watch Netflix instead.

Solutions: find a coding buddy — someone at the same level. You help each other stay on track. Join communities like r/learnprogramming. Show your work publicly on GitHub.

Getting assessed matters too — objective feedback shows you your real skill level and removes both imposter syndrome AND false confidence. Only what's measured can be improved.

TL;DR — Your ActionTell someone about your goal. Post your progress online. Find a study buddy at AIT. Commit publicly.
08
Rule Eight
Keep Learning
+

Being a good programmer is like being Madonna — the world keeps evolving, and you have to keep reinventing yourself. New frameworks, new languages, new trends appear constantly.

Great programmers relish learning new things, even if it means becoming a beginner again. Don't be the person still writing Fortran in 2024 because they refused to learn anything else.

The core programming concepts (loops, functions, logic) you learn now will last forever. The specific tools will change — and that's fine. Learning your second language is always much faster than the first.

TL;DR — Your ActionAfter finishing this bootcamp, pick ONE new thing to learn next — whether it's mobile (React Native), AI (Python), or another area.
09
Rule Nine
Play Foosball
+

Real programmers don't look like Hollywood hackers rapidly smashing keyboards. They mostly look like they're… staring at nothing. A lot of staring.

When you're stuck on a bug, sometimes the worst thing you can do is keep staring at it. Step away. Sleep on it. Take a walk. Play a game. In 9 out of 10 cases, the solution will appear when you stop forcing it.

The easiest code to delete is the code that was never written. Code less, think more. Poorly thought-out code written quickly always requires painful refactoring later.

TL;DR — Your ActionWhen you've been stuck for more than 30 minutes: take a break. Walk around. Come back fresh. The answer will often appear immediately.
10
Rule Ten
Get a Mentor
+

Pair programming: a learner writes code while a mentor reviews each line in real time. You can tap into decades of accumulated wisdom in just a few hours. It's uncomfortable at first — but enormously valuable.

Good mentors don't just give you the answer — they ask questions that get you to think for yourself (the Socratic method). Learning to approach a problem is more valuable than memorising solutions.

How to find a mentor: go to meetup.com and find tech meetups. Offer your skills in exchange for theirs. Don't say "be my mentor" — just offer to help them in return for their help.

TL;DR — Your ActionFind a senior student or developer at AIT. Offer to help with something they need. Exchange skills. You have more to offer than you think.
11
Rule Eleven
Get into the Habit of Chunking
+

Got a big, complicated idea? It feels impossible because you're looking at it as one giant thing. The solution: chunking — break it into smaller and smaller pieces until each piece is solvable.

Example: building a "butter the toast robot" sounds complex. But broken down: (1) pick up toast and position it, (2) pick up butter, (3) spread butter on toast. Each piece is now solvable. Then break those down further.

This is the single most important programming skill: taking a huge problem and breaking it into bite-sized chunks. Practice it on every project.

TL;DR — Your ActionBefore writing any code, write out your plan in plain English. Break the problem into 3-5 sub-problems. Then break each sub-problem down again.
12
Rule Twelve
Break Someone Else's Code
+

Don't just copy-paste code from Stack Overflow and move on. That teaches you nothing. Next time you see the same problem in a different context, you'll be stuck again.

Instead, when you find code that solves your problem:

Step 1 — Paste it and confirm it works. Step 2 — Delete it line by line. Each time you delete a line, observe what breaks and why. Step 3 — Swap lines around. Does order matter? Why? Step 4 — Try to rewrite it from scratch without looking.

Advanced version: go to GitHub, find a small open-source project, run it, study its structure, then rebuild it from scratch. Compare your version to the original.

TL;DR — Your ActionThe next time you copy code from Stack Overflow: delete it line by line and understand every single line before moving on.
"Move fast and break things." — Mark Zuckerberg
